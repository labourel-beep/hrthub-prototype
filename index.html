Menopause & women's health

Your first three months on HRT: what to expect

By · 6 min read

Starting hormone therapy is rarely an overnight switch. Most people notice changes in stages over the first few months as your body settles into a steady level. Here's an honest timeline so you know what's normal — and what's worth a call to your prescriber.

The first few weeks

Hot flashes and night sweats are often the first symptoms to ease, sometimes within two to three weeks. Sleep may start to improve as night sweats settle. It's also common to feel very little at first, or to have minor, temporary side effects like breast tenderness, mild bloating, or spotting as your body adjusts. These usually fade.

Weeks four to eight

This is often when the bigger picture comes into focus. Mood, energy, and mental clarity tend to shift more gradually than hot flashes. If you're using a patch or gel, your prescriber may fine-tune the dose around this point based on how you feel — small adjustments are a normal part of getting it right, not a sign something is wrong.

Months two to three

By now many people have a clearer sense of what's working. Vaginal and urinary symptoms, which respond more slowly, often continue improving through this window and beyond. If you have a uterus and are taking estrogen, your prescriber will have you on a progestogen to protect the uterine lining, and they'll talk through any bleeding patterns.

When to check in

Reach out sooner rather than later if symptoms aren't budging at all, if side effects are bothering you, or if you have heavy or persistent bleeding, a severe headache, or leg swelling or pain. These are worth a prompt conversation.

Key takeaways
  • Hot flashes often ease first; mood, energy, and clarity take longer.
  • Mild early side effects usually settle within weeks.
  • Dose adjustments are normal — expect a check-in around 6–8 weeks.
  • Heavy bleeding or severe symptoms warrant a prompt call.

Menopause & women's health

Perimenopause vs. menopause, explained

By · 5 min read

These two words get used interchangeably, but they describe different stages — and knowing which one you're in helps make sense of your symptoms and your options.

What perimenopause is

Perimenopause is the transition leading up to menopause. It can start in your early 40s (sometimes late 30s) and last several years. Hormone levels — especially estrogen — don't simply decline; they fluctuate, often unpredictably. That's why periods become irregular and why symptoms like hot flashes, sleep disruption, mood changes, and brain fog can come and go.

What menopause is

Menopause is a single point in time: 12 consecutive months after your last period. After that you're post-menopausal. By this stage, hormone production has largely settled at a lower, steadier level, which is why some symptoms change in character.

Why the symptoms differ

In perimenopause, the fluctuation itself drives a lot of the turbulence — good weeks and bad weeks. Post-menopause, symptoms are more about the sustained lower estrogen level, and things like vaginal dryness or bone health become more relevant over time.

What helps at each stage

Both stages can be supported. In perimenopause, care often focuses on smoothing out symptoms and protecting sleep and mood; some people use hormonal birth control here. Post-menopause, hormone therapy is a common, effective option for many — the right choice depends on your symptoms, history, and preferences.

Key takeaways
  • Perimenopause = the fluctuating transition; menopause = 12 months after your last period.
  • Irregular periods and "good/bad weeks" are hallmarks of perimenopause.
  • Care is available at every stage — it just looks a little different.

Menopause & women's health

Is it ever too late to start HRT?

By · 5 min read

It's one of the most common questions we hear — and the honest answer is that it's less about a hard cutoff and more about weighing benefits and risks for you.

The "timing" idea

Research suggests that starting hormone therapy nearer to the menopause transition — generally under age 60 or within about ten years of your last period — tends to have a more favorable balance of benefits to risks, particularly for symptom relief and bone health. This is often called the "timing hypothesis."

Starting later

Starting further out isn't automatically off the table, but the considerations shift, and some risks can be higher. It becomes a more individual decision that weighs your symptoms, your cardiovascular and other health history, and the type and route of hormones.

It's a conversation, not a formula

There's no single number that applies to everyone. The type of estrogen, whether it's a patch or pill, the dose, and whether you need a progestogen all change the picture. That's exactly the kind of nuance a specialist is there to work through with you.

Key takeaways
  • Starting closer to the transition generally has a more favorable risk-benefit balance.
  • Later starts are individual decisions, not automatic "no"s.
  • Type, dose, and route all matter — a specialist tailors it.

Men's hormonal health

Low testosterone: signs, testing, and treatment

By · 6 min read

Testosterone naturally declines with age, but a meaningfully low level — combined with symptoms — is what clinicians call hypogonadism. Here's how it's recognized and what care looks like.

Common signs

Symptoms can include low energy and motivation, reduced libido, erectile changes, low mood, loss of muscle or increased body fat, and poor sleep. None of these is specific to testosterone on its own, which is why testing matters before treatment.

How it's diagnosed

Diagnosis generally needs two separate morning blood tests (testosterone is highest in the morning) showing a low level, alongside symptoms. Your clinician may also check related markers such as LH, FSH, SHBG, and prolactin to understand the cause, and rule out other explanations.

Treatment options

If therapy is appropriate, testosterone can be given as a topical gel, an injection, or other forms. The goal is to bring levels into a healthy range and improve symptoms — not to chase the highest possible number. Choice of form comes down to your labs, lifestyle, and preference.

What's monitored

Once on therapy, your clinician tracks your testosterone level, red blood cell count (hematocrit), and PSA, along with how you feel, adjusting as needed.

Key takeaways
  • Symptoms plus two low morning tests are the usual basis for diagnosis.
  • Treatment aims for a healthy range, not a maximum.
  • Ongoing monitoring (testosterone, hematocrit, PSA) is part of safe care.

Men's hormonal health

What testosterone therapy does — and doesn't do

By · 5 min read

Testosterone therapy can make a real difference for men with genuinely low levels and symptoms — but expectations matter. Here's a grounded look at what tends to change.

What often improves

When low testosterone is the cause, many men notice better energy, libido, mood, and over time changes in body composition (more muscle, less fat with activity). Improvements are gradual and build over months.

Realistic timelines

Libido and mood can shift within weeks. Energy follows. Changes in muscle and body fat take several months and still depend on exercise and nutrition — therapy isn't a substitute for those.

What it won't fix

If your testosterone is normal, adding more is unlikely to help and can cause harm. Testosterone isn't a treatment for general aging, and it won't resolve symptoms that have another cause. It can also reduce fertility, which matters if you're planning to have children.

Staying safe

Therapy calls for periodic bloodwork and follow-up. Done well, it's about restoring a healthy level and feeling like yourself — monitored along the way.

Key takeaways
  • Biggest gains are for men who are genuinely low and symptomatic.
  • Libido/mood shift in weeks; body composition takes months.
  • It can affect fertility and needs ongoing monitoring.

Gender-affirming care

Starting gender-affirming hormone therapy

By · 6 min read

Gender-affirming hormone therapy (GAHT) helps align your body with your gender. Care in our network follows WPATH standards, and the path is collaborative — you and your clinician decide together.

Before you start

A clinician will talk through your goals, health history, and any fertility considerations, and usually order baseline labs. This is also the time to ask questions about what changes to expect and on what timeline.

The first months

Some effects begin within weeks to a few months, while others develop over a year or more. Early changes are often subtle — skin, mood, libido — before the more visible changes follow. Patience is part of the process, and timelines vary by person and regimen.

Monitoring

Your clinician checks hormone levels and relevant health markers periodically, especially in the first year, and adjusts doses to balance your goals with safety.

Support along the way

Many people find that emotional and community support matters as much as the medical side. Our care team can help connect you with a clinician experienced in gender-affirming care, whether you're starting fresh or transferring an existing regimen.

Key takeaways
  • Care follows WPATH standards and is decided collaboratively.
  • Some changes start in weeks; others take a year or more.
  • Regular monitoring guides safe dose adjustments.

Gender-affirming care

Feminizing vs. masculinizing therapy: an overview

By · 5 min read

Gender-affirming hormone therapy is usually grouped into feminizing and masculinizing approaches. Here's a plain-language overview of what each involves.

Feminizing therapy

This typically combines estrogen with a medication that lowers or blocks testosterone's effects (an anti-androgen). Expected changes can include softer skin, breast development, redistribution of body fat, and reduced body hair over time. Effects build gradually over months to a couple of years.

Masculinizing therapy

This centers on testosterone. Expected changes can include a deeper voice, increased body and facial hair, fat redistribution, muscle development, and cessation of periods. As with feminizing therapy, the timeline unfolds over months to years.

What's reversible

Some effects are reversible if therapy stops; others — like voice deepening with testosterone, or breast growth with estrogen — are generally permanent. A clinician will walk through which is which before you start, so your choices are fully informed.

Key takeaways
  • Feminizing = estrogen + anti-androgen; masculinizing = testosterone.
  • Changes develop gradually over months to years.
  • Some effects are permanent — your clinician explains which before starting.

For everyone

How to actually read your hormone labs

By · 5 min read

Lab results can look intimidating, but a few principles make them far easier to understand — and to discuss with your specialist.

Ranges aren't verdicts

The "reference range" beside each result is a statistical band for a general population — not a personal target. A value just inside or outside the range isn't automatically fine or alarming; what matters is your result in the context of your symptoms, age, and goals.

A few key markers

  • Estradiol — the main estrogen; central to menopause and feminizing therapy.
  • Testosterone — measured as total and sometimes free; relevant across all three areas of care.
  • FSH & LH — signaling hormones that help describe where you are in a transition.
  • SHBG — affects how much hormone is "free" and active, which is why free levels can differ from totals.

Timing matters

Hormones fluctuate by time of day and, for menstruating people, by cycle day. Testosterone is checked in the morning; some estrogen tests depend on cycle timing. A single number is a snapshot, not the whole story.

Bring it to your specialist

The most useful thing you can do is review results together with how you actually feel. That combination — numbers plus symptoms — is what guides good decisions.

Key takeaways
  • Reference ranges are population bands, not personal targets.
  • Free vs. total levels can differ because of SHBG.
  • Timing affects results — context beats any single number.

Member story

"I finally feel like myself again"

A member story · reviewed by · 4 min read

For two years, Dana put her symptoms down to stress. The hot flashes were one thing, but it was the 3 a.m. wake-ups and the fog at work that wore her down. "I kept being told my labs were 'normal,' so I assumed I just had to push through," she says.

A friend mentioned she'd found a specialist who actually spent time on the conversation. Dana booked a video visit. "The difference was that someone looked at the whole picture — my symptoms, my history, what I wanted — not just one number on a page."

She started a low-dose patch and a progestogen, with a check-in scheduled for six weeks later. The night sweats eased first. By the second month, the fog had lifted enough that she noticed it was gone. "It wasn't dramatic. It was more like the volume on everything that had been wrong slowly turned down."

Dana's experience is her own, and not everyone's path looks the same. But the part she most wants others to hear is simple: "If something feels off, keep asking. Feeling like yourself again is worth the conversation."

If this sounds familiar
  • "Normal" labs don't always mean nothing can help.
  • A specialist who reviews the whole picture makes a difference.
  • Improvements are often gradual — give it a few months and a follow-up.

Cyrille Labourel

Cyrille Labourel is a healthcare executive and entrepreneur dedicated to making hormone therapy more accessible. As founder and CEO of The HRT Club, he created a new access platform that connects patients, prescribers, and manufacturers to deliver expert, transparent, insurance-free hormonal care. Cyrille brings over 20 years of experience from Merck, Organon, Johnson & Johnson, and Besins Healthcare USA, where he also serves as Managing Director & CEO. Lauren Darisse is the General Counsel and Chief Operating Officer of The HRT Club, where she built the legal and operational infrastructure from the ground up — turning a vision for accessible, insurance-free hormone therapy into a compliant, high-trust platform now serving thousands nationwide.
